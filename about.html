The Genesis of Earthsong

Founded in 2012 by master gardener Dr. Elara Thorne and agroecologist Marcel Voss, Earthsong Arboria Studio emerged from a decade of research into climate-resilient permaculture. What began as a half‑acre experimental plot on Hawkmeadow Valley has blossomed into a 47‑acre living laboratory and design studio. Our name honours the ancient belief that healthy soil emits a low-frequency hum — mycelial vibrations that connect every root and leaf. We started with a simple credo: “Listen beneath the brown, celebrate the green, and design with succession, not control.”
Today, our studio stewards three main bioregional hubs: the Appalachian teaching forest, the Pacific fog prairie project, and the arid‑lands regenerative orchard in the Southwest. We’ve worked with over 340 private estates, public parks, and indigenous land‑back initiatives, always prioritising native ecologies and heirloom genetics. At our core, we are soil builders, habitat weavers, and storytellers of the photosynthetic world. Our approach merges hard data — soil carbon fractions, water infiltration rates — with intuitive design that feels timeless and enchanted.

Our mission transcends aesthetics: we aim to reverse topsoil depletion by demonstrating that ornamental gardens can also function as carbon sinks, pollinator refuges, and food sources. Every Earthsong design includes a minimum of 60% native keystone genera (oaks, willows, asters, goldenrods). And we never treat soil as mere dirt — we treat it as a living community. By integrating fungal networks, silvopasture, and rainwater harvesting, we’ve helped turn barren lawns into self‑regulating Edens. Our name has become synonymous with the “brown‑green revolution” — not a fleeting trend but a return to deep ecological literacy.

The Brown-Green Covenant

We see brown not as lifeless dirt but as the pantry of eternity — decomposing leaves, woody debris, humus, and fungal highways. Green is the breath of creation: chlorophyll converting sunlight into sugar, roots exuding carbon feeds, and canopies dancing with birds. Our studio’s central covenant: increase the brown to grow the green, and let the green return as brown. This circle of fertility is our foundational design principle.
In practice, we apply this covenant through yearly “Soil Sabbath” rituals, where we refrain from tilling and instead sheet-mulch with cardboard, wood chips, and green hay. We monitor soil respiration, mycorrhizal colonization rates, and invertebrate biodiversity. This deep ecology has led to the discovery of rare orchid populations returning to our terraced woodlands after a decade of neglect. For us, brown is memory, green is hope — together they create a sanctuary that outlives any single generation.
Our team has also pioneered the “Earthy Palette Design System” — a framework that uses local geology and native plant assemblages to create resilient, low‑maintenance gardens. Each palette features a “brown anchor” (e.g., an ancient stone bench, a sculptural dead snag, a berm of ramial chips) and a “green heart” (a keystone shrub or canopy tree). From there, perennials and groundcovers weave the tapestry. The result: gardens that look like they have always belonged, welcoming both solitude and wild abundance.

Landmarks of Regeneration

🌲 The Hawkmeadow Arboretum

Our flagship site, a former corn mono‑crop, transformed into a 20‑acre silvopasture and nut orchard. More than 180 varieties of chestnut, hickory, and oak now thrive here. Annual mushroom yields exceed 6,000 lbs, and soil organic matter rose from 1.2% to 6.8% in nine years.

🌊 Tidal Roots Project (Coastal Maine)

A collaboration with coastal land trusts to create salt‑tolerant food hedges using beach plum, bayberry, and sea kale. Brown wrack seaweed used as mulch builds salt‑marsh edge resilience while green dune grasses stabilise erosion.

🏜️ Sonoran Edible Oasis

In the arid Southwest, we installed passive rainwater basins, shade structures, and a palette of native desert perennials — prickly pear, mesquite, and desert willow. The brown earth (caliche) was amended with biochar and local compost, increasing water retention by 300%.

🕯️ The Mycelial Memorial Garden

A contemplative space where visitors walk through log‑layered paths inoculated with oyster and reishi fungi. A living tribute to species lost to climate change, designed with brown remembrance and green resilience.
These projects embody our commitment to open-source knowledge: we publish detailed construction guides, plant lists, and monitoring data on our digital library. To date, over 12,000 regenerative practitioners have adopted methods first prototyped at Earthsong. We also offer a mentorship program for aspiring ecological designers, bridging the gap between academic knowledge and hands‑on craft. The brown‑green legacy is not owned — it’s shared.

The Seventh Generation Promise

Inspired by Indigenous Haudenosaunee philosophy, we evaluate every decision by its impact on the next seven generations. That means we refuse to use synthetic herbicides, neonics, or peat moss (which destroys ancient carbon sinks). Instead, we propagate plants in coir and compost, rely on integrated pest management with beneficial insects, and prioritise plant genetics that adapt to changing climates. Each landscape we design includes an “eternal soil” clause — a legally binding covenant that future owners must maintain the soil food web and never revert to chemical monoculture. As of 2026, all 47 Earthsong‑designed properties remain in full regenerative compliance.
📜 Land Back Initiatives: We’ve helped return 86 acres of previously cultivated land to indigenous stewardship, with our design team offering free ecological transition plans. We believe that healing the land is inseparable from justice and sovereignty.
♻️ Closed‑Loop Nursery: Our on‑site nursery grows 40,000+ native and heirloom plants every year, using rainwater, solar‑powered fans, and biocontainers made from mushroom mycelium. Nothing goes to landfill.
The Earthsong Arboria Studio is also a designated Monarch Waystation and a “Wildlife Friendly Certified” habitat. We host monthly forest bathing walks, carbon farming workshops, and free seed libraries. Visitors often remark that our gardens feel like a cathedral of calm — where the brown soil smells of damp earth after rain, and the green leaves whisper ancient patterns. This is our offer: a future where gardening is not a chore but a ceremony, and where every backyard can become a micro‑sanctuary.
Our 2030 vision: to establish regional “Arboria Hubs” across four bioregions, training 1,000 new regenerative designers and stewarding 10,000 acres into vibrant brown‑green mosaics. Each hub will combine an edible nursery, a seed bank, and a mycology lab. We have already secured initial funding from the TerraGenesis Fund, and we invite you to be part of this living movement — whether by planting an oak, hosting a workshop, or simply letting a corner of your lawn go wild. The earth remembers; we only need to listen.
